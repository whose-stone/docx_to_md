UNITED STATES SUPREME COURT

**Gonzalez v. Trevino**
US Supreme Court
June 20, 2024
Docket: 22-1025

*LEO LAW Topic **9-4**:**  Evidence showing that no one had ever been arrested for similar conduct was permissible to fall within the Nieves exception in a civil suit for First Amendment retaliation.*

Sylvia Gonzalez, a city council member in Castle Hills, Texas, was involved in a campaign to remove the city manager, Ryan Rapelye. She gathered signatures for a petition introduced at a city council meeting. After the meeting, the mayor, Edward Trevino, asked Gonzalez for the petition. Gonzalez found the petition in her binder, which she claimed was unintentional. Trevino reported this to the city police, leading to an investigation. A private attorney concluded that Gonzalez likely violated a Texas anti-tampering statute, leading to her arrest. The charges were later dismissed, but Gonzalez claimed this incident led her to step away from politics.

Gonzalez filed a lawsuit under 42 USC §1983 in Federal District Court against Mayor Trevino, the police chief, and the private attorney, alleging that her arrest was in retaliation for her role in the petition and violated her First Amendment rights. The defendants filed a motion to dismiss the suit. The District Court denied the motion to dismiss the complaint, finding that it fell within an exception to the no-probable-cause rule recognized in *Nieves v. Bartlett*, 587 U. S. 391, 402 (2019). However, the Fifth Circuit reversed this decision, stating that Gonzalez's claim could only fall within the *Nieves* exception if she provided "comparative evidence" of similarly situated individuals who engaged in the same criminal conduct but were not arrested.

The United States Supreme Court disagreed with the Fifth Circuit's interpretation of the *Nieves* exception. The Supreme Court found that the Fifth Circuit's requirement for specific comparator evidence was overly restrictive. The court clarified that to fall within the Nieves exception, a plaintiff must produce objective evidence to prove that his arrest occurred in circumstances where officers typically exercise their discretion not to make arrests. The court held that Gonzalez's evidence, showing that no one had ever been arrested for similar conduct, was permissible. The court vacated the Fifth Circuit's judgment and remanded the case for further proceedings.

**Chiaverini v. City of Napoleon**
U.S. Supreme Court
June 20, 2024
Docket: 23-50

*LEO LAW Topic** 9-4**:** The presence of probable cause for one charge in a criminal proceeding does not categorically defeat a Fourth Amendment malicious prosecution claim relating to another baseless charge.*

Jascha Chiaverini was the manager of the Diamond and Gold Outlet in Napoleon, Ohio. He bought a men's ring and diamond earring from Brent Burns for $45. He recorded the transaction, including copying Burns' ID and photographing the items. After the purchase, David and Christina Hill contacted Chiaverini and claimed the jewelry was stolen from them. Chiaverini advised them to report to the police but denied buying the items they described. After multiple calls, Chiaverini ended the conversation. Both parties contacted the police. Chiaverini expressed his suspicion about holding stolen property and requested that police, not the Hills, visit. When the police arrived, Chiaverini cooperated, providing information and photographs of the jewelry.

The situation escalated when Chiaverini received a conflicting "hold letter" from the police, instructing him to keep the items as evidence but also instructed him to release them to the Hills. Chiaverini refused to release the items, citing legal concerns and advice from his counsel. As a result of his meeting with Police Chief Weitzel, it was discovered that Chiaverini did not have a precious metal dealer license. As a result, the police initiated a new investigation. Officer Steward updated the police report to include Chiaverini's suspicion about the stolen nature of the items, which Chiaverini disputed. Based on these developments, warrants were issued for Chiaverini's arrest and the search of his store, leading to his temporary detention. After a court dismissed the criminal case against Chiaverini, he filed a complaint against the officers and the city, alleging various constitutional violations. 

The District Court granted summary judgment to the officers, and the Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit affirmed. The Court of Appeals held that Chiaverini's detention and prosecution were supported by probable cause. In its decision, the court did not address whether the officers had probable cause to bring the money-laundering charge. The court believed that there was clearly probable cause to charge Chiaverini with the two misdemeanors. The Sixth Circuit held that a malicious prosecution claim based on any other charge would fail as long as one of the charges was supported by probable cause.

Before the Supreme Court, the question was: Should a Fourth Amendment malicious-prosecution claim proceed as to a baseless criminal charge so long as other charges brought alongside the baseless charge are supported by probable cause?

The Supreme Court of the United States held that the presence of probable cause for one charge in a criminal proceeding does not categorically defeat a Fourth Amendment malicious prosecution claim relating to another baseless charge. Accordingly, the Supreme Court vacated the judgment of the Court of Appeals and remanded the case for further proceedings consistent with its opinion.

FIFTH CIRCUIT

**Hall v. Trochessett**
U.S. Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit
June 20, 2024
Docket: 23-40362

*LEO LAW Topic 1-5, **9-4**: Under the independent intermediary doctrine, if the** truthful and relevant** facts supporting an arrest are put before an intermediary **(**such as a magistrate or grand jury**)**, the intermediary's decision to issue a warrant or return an indictment breaks the causal chain and insulates the initiating party from** tort** liabil**i**ty** for false arrest or malicious prosecution**. *

Randall Hall's wife was involved in a minor car accident. The other driver reported the incident as a hit-and-run, leading to an investigation by Officer Trochesset. When Trochesset arrived at the Halls' home to gather information, Hall, who was not present, instructed his wife over the phone not to provide the requested information to Trochesset. As a result, Trochesset obtained an arrest warrant for Hall for interfering with public duties, and Hall was arrested. The District Attorney dropped the charges. Hall filed a 42 USC §1983 federal civil rights suit against Officer Trochesset and the City of League City, Texas, alleging constitutional violations following his arrest for interference with a police investigation. The district court dismissed Hall's suit, ruling in favor of Trochesset and the City of League City. Hall appealed the decision.

The Fifth Circuit affirmed the lower court's decision. The court found that probable cause existed for Hall's arrest, as Hall had interfered with Trochesset's investigation. The court also applied the independent intermediary doctrine, which states that an officer who presents all relevant facts to an impartial intermediary (in this case, a justice of the peace) is not liable if the intermediary's independent decision leads to an arrest. Notably, the court found that Trochesset had not provided false information or withheld any relevant information from the justice of the peace. Furthermore, the court ruled that Hall failed to establish that Trochesset violated the Fourth or Fourteenth Amendments. The court also dismissed Hall's claim against the City of League City, as there was no constitutional violation by Trochesset, and Hall failed to identify an official policy or custom that led to the alleged violation. 

**U****nited States**** v. Black**
U.S. Court of Appeals for the Seventh Circuit
June 20, 2024
Docket: 22-2659

*LEO LAW Topic** 6-10**:**  Officers had the reasonable suspicion necessary for the warrantless detention of a package, and the one-day detention while seeking a search warrant was not unreasonable.*

Law enforcement officers intercepted a package addressed to Roland Black, believing it contained narcotics. After obtaining a warrant, they found the substance, replaced it with sham narcotics, and delivered the package to Black's residence. Black was arrested after the package was opened and he was found with luminescent powder from the sham narcotics on his hands. Black was charged with attempting to possess with intent to distribute a controlled substance. Prior to his trial, Black moved to suppress all evidence derived from the seizure of the package, arguing the officers lacked reasonable suspicion to seize the package. The district court denied the motion, ruling that under a totality of the circumstances, the officers had the requisite reasonable suspicion.

Black appealed to the Seventh Circuit, contending that the officers lacked reasonable suspicion to seize the package. The court noted that law enforcement officers may detain a package for a reasonable length of time while investigating it if there is reasonable suspicion that the package contains contraband. The court found that the officers had reasonable suspicion to seize the package based on their knowledge of criminal history, previous packages with contraband sent to the address, and fleeing a state to avoid prosecution. The court also held that the one-day length of the detention was not unreasonable. Accordingly, the lower court's denial of the motion to suppress was affirmed. 

**United States v. Austin**
Court of Appeals for the Eighth Circuit
June 17, 2024
Docket: 23-2570

*LEO LAW Topics 5-1, 10-3: Reasonable suspicion of criminal activity justified the extension of the traffic stop, and the subsequent Terry Frisk of the suspect was reasonable.*

Reginald Austin was stopped for a traffic violation in St. Louis. The officers believed he was in the same vehicle they had seen driving recklessly earlier that day. Upon approaching the car, the officers observed the driver making movements that suggested he was hiding something under the seat. When asking for identification, one officer smelled marijuana and noticed a suspicious bulge in his pants. Discovering Austin was a felon, he was ordered out of the vehicle and frisked. When Austin made a movement that suggested he was reaching for a concealed item, an officer grabbed his arm. At this point, officers attempted to remove a cell phone from Austin's hand. Austin spontaneously shouted to the person on the cell phone: "They're locking me up. They found the gun, baby." A second pat-down search revealed a loaded handgun, and officers placed Austin under arrest for unlawful possession of a firearm.

At trial, Austin moved to suppress the firearm, arguing that the officers unreasonably prolonged the traffic stop and lacked reasonable suspicion to conduct the pat-down searches. The district court denied Austin's motion to suppress the firearm, holding the traffic stop was not unreasonably prolonged, and the officers had reasonable suspicion to conduct a protective pat-down. Austin appealed the denial of his motion to suppress. 

The United States Court of Appeals for the Eighth Circuit affirmed the district court's decision. First, the court found that the traffic stop was not unreasonably prolonged. The odor of marijuana, Austin's furtive behaviors suggestive of an attempt to conceal something, and his admission that he had smoked marijuana earlier that day gave the officers reasonable suspicion to prolong the traffic stop. Second, the officers had reasonable suspicion to conduct a protective pat-down frisk based on the furtive movements, the suspicious bulge in his pants, the smell of marijuana, and the fact Austin was a felon. 

**Bulfin v. Rainwater**
U.S. Court of Appeals for the Eighth Circuit
June 17, 2024
Docket: 23-2357

*LEO LAW Topic** 5-19**: **G**overnment officials** had the** consent of a person with apparent authority **to seize and destroy **an effect **(dog) and, therefore, did not violate the Fourth Amendment.** *

Erin Bulfin brought a 42 USC §1983 civil suit against St. Louis County and several employees of St. Louis County Animal Care & Control (ACC) after her pet dog, Daisy, was euthanized by A.C.C. Bulfin's dog had bitten her daughter in the face, inflicting injuries that required hospitalization. Following the incident, Bulfin's husband, Edward Nea, took Daisy to ACC, where he signed a form authorizing the euthanasia of Daisy. When Bulfin went to ACC to pick up Daisy the following day, she was informed that Daisy had been euthanized. The district court granted summary judgment to the individual defendants, concluding that Bulfin's Fourth Amendment rights were not violated because the defendants reasonably relied on the apparent consent of Bulfin's husband to euthanize Daisy. The court also granted summary judgment in favor of the County on Bulfin's federal municipal liability claims.

The Eighth Circuit affirmed the district court's decision. The court held that when ACC accepted custody of Daisy, it was not a Fourth Amendment seizure because Bulfin approved of Daisy being quarantined in ACC custody after biting her daughter. The court concluded that no Fourth Amendment violation occurred by seizing and destroying an effect with the consent of a person with apparent authority.

**U****nited States**** v. Harden**
U.S. Court of Appeals for the Eleventh Circuit
June 18, 2024
Docket: 20-14004

*LEO LAW Topic 5-30: A** warrantless search of a probationer's home, based on reasonable suspicion and a probation condition allowing warrantless searches, is not** made** unreasonable because the ho**us**e **is **also occupied by another person who knew about the probation conditions.*

The constitutional issue in this case is the warrantless search of a probationer's home, which the probationer and his non-probationer girlfriend occupied. The probationer, Tremayne Linder, was on probation for burglary and attempted armed robbery. The conditional probation allowed for warrantless searches of his residence. Lakesia Harden, Linder's girlfriend, was aware of his probation status. The police, suspecting marijuana use, conducted a warrantless search of Linder's home and found marijuana and methamphetamine in a shared closet. Harden was subsequently arrested and charged with possession with intent to distribute the drugs.

Before trial, Harden moved to suppress the drugs and her post-arrest statements as fruits of the allegedly unlawful search. The district court denied the suppression motions. At trial, the government admitted the drugs and Harden's statements into evidence, and the jury found her guilty as charged in the indictment. Harden appealed the denial of her suppression motions.

The Eleventh Circuit affirmed the district court decision. It held that a warrantless search of a probationer's home, based on reasonable suspicion and a probation condition allowing warrantless searches, is not rendered unreasonable because the house is also occupied by another person who knew about the probation conditions. 

**STATE CASES**

**People v. Johnson**
Court: Colorado Supreme Court
June 17, 2024

*LEO LAW Topic 10-3: Officers developed probable cause to arrest during a traffic stop and did not unlawfully prolong the stop by calling for a drug K9 to sniff the vehicle.*

On the afternoon of April 12, 2023, the defendant, Sean Terrance Johnson, was pulled over by police officers for making turns without signaling. During the stop, officers noticed an empty shell casing in the front seat cupholder. The casing raised the officers' suspicions. They also noticed that Johnson, who appeared unusually nervous, was wearing a camouflage bag strapped to his chest, a folding knife clipped to it, and a bag often used to carry concealed weapons. When asked about firearms in the vehicle, Johnson admitted to having a shotgun in the trunk. After initially refusing to exit the car, Johnson was frisked. The frisk revealed a pipe in Johnson's pocket, which he admitted to smoking meth the previous night. Based on these findings, the officers arrested Johnson. A drug K9 alerted on the vehicle, which was searched, yielding drugs in the car and two shotguns in the trunk. 

Before trial, Johnson moved to suppress the evidence found in his car, contending that the officers subjected him to an investigative detention unsupported by reasonable suspicion, which led to his formal arrest without probable cause or a warrant in violation of his Fourth Amendment rights. The district court initially suppressed the evidence found in Johnson's vehicle, ruling that the officers had unlawfully prolonged their investigatory stop by waiting for a drug-detection dog to arrive. 

The Colorado Supreme Court disagreed with the lower court's decision. The court found that the officers had probable cause to arrest Johnson for possession of drug paraphernalia. Thus, his continued detention was justified as an arrest, not an investigatory stop. The court concluded that the officers did not unlawfully prolong their investigatory stop of Johnson. Therefore, the Supreme Court reversed the district court's order suppressing the evidence discovered in Johnson's vehicle and remanded the case for the court to consider whether that evidence was lawfully discovered following Johnson's arrest.

**Dhyne v. People**
Colorado Supreme Court
June 17, 2024

*LEO LAW Topic **4-3**:** Authority of a search warrant extended to a basement apartment that did not outwardly appear to be separate from the home and used the same internet IP address.*

Police obtained a warrant to search a property after receiving information that child pornography was downloaded to a particular internet protocol (IP) address associated with that property. Outwardly, the property appeared to be a single-family home. However, during the execution of the warrant, the police encountered Kevin Matthew Dhyne, who lived in a basement apartment on the property and used the same internet access as the rest of the house. The police searched Dhyne's apartment and found sexually explicit material involving children on his laptop.

At trial, Dhyne argued the search violated the US and Colorado constitutions because the warrant was not specific to his basement apartment. However, the court denied Dhyne's motion to suppress the evidence, reasoning that even if the officers had not searched his apartment in conjunction with the original warrant, they would have executed the search later that day under a warrant specific to the basement apartment, and the evidence would therefore have been inevitably discovered. Dhyne was convicted of two counts of sexual exploitation of a child.

The Colorado Court of Appeals affirmed the trial court's denial of the suppression motion, but did so by upholding the search rather than by applying the inevitable discovery exception. The court of appeals agreed that separate dwellings normally require separate, specific warrants for a multi-dwelling unit. However, the court justified the search of Dhyne's apartment based on the shared use of the IP address.

The Supreme Court of the State of Colorado affirmed the outcome, holding that the warrant's reference to the property's house, garage, and any outbuildings was sufficiently specific because there were no outward indicators that the basement apartment existed. The court also held that the execution of the warrant was reasonable in this particular scenario, where the warrant was for all buildings on the property, and the defendant told the police that he lived in the basement and used the IP address that provided grounds for the search.

**State v. Lanchantin**
Montana Supreme Court
June 18, 2024

*LEO LAW Topic 1-1:  Under Montana state law, which **provide**s individuals more protection than the Fourth Amendment, law enforcement cannot enter** private property without a warrant** or exigent circumstances. *

A Montana Highway Patrol Trooper turned on his lights to pull over Neil Lanchantin for speeding. Lanchantin pulled onto a private road, posted "no trespassing," and stopped in front of a residence where Lanchantin was residing with his girlfriend. After a short interaction, the Trooper arrested Lanchantin for his felony DUI. 

At trial, Lanchantin filed a motion to suppress, arguing the Trooper violated his Fourth Amendment rights by entering the private property without a warrant or a judicially recognized exception. The trial court denied the motion, and Lanchantin appealed. The court denied Lanchantin's motion to suppress, holding he did not have a reasonable expectation of privacy at the location of the stop. Lanchantin pled guilty to the DUI charge but reserved his right to appeal. 

On appeal to the Montana Supreme Court, the State argued that Lanchantin had no privacy interest in the property because he did not live there and that the "No Trespassing" signs were inadequate to convey an expectation of privacy because they were tacked to trees along the side of the road. The Montana Supreme Court reversed the District Court's decision, holding that Lanchantin had a reasonable expectation of privacy in the driveway of the property where he was residing with his girlfriend. The court found that the "No Trespassing" signs were sufficient to manifest an actual expectation of privacy that society recognizes as reasonable. The court concluded that, under Montana law, law enforcement cannot enter onto private property without a warrant, even in the case of a misdemeanant fleeing, unless exigent circumstances exist. The case was remanded for further proceedings consistent with the court's opinion.

**People v Wright**
New York Court of Appeals
June 18, 2024

*LEO LAW Topic 9-11: Show-up identification was not unduly suggestive because it was made very close in proximity and time to** the incident.*

On March 26, 2017, two men robbed a restaurant in Queens. One of the robbers, a Black man wearing a red hoodie, black jacket, and a bandana over his face, approached the Assistant Manager, Sumintra Ramsahoye, and demanded that she open the safe. When she couldn't, she handed over the contents of the cash register drawers. Meanwhile, the other perpetrator stood next to another employee, Jordan Guzman. After the robbers fled, Guzman called the police. Police Officer Bryce Blake and his partner arrived at the restaurant within minutes and observed the defendant, Freddie T. Wright, standing in the parking lot dressed in a black jacket and red hoodie. Wright fled upon seeing the patrol car, but was eventually arrested. Guzman and Ramsahoye were brought to the house where Wright was arrested to identify him in a "show up" identification procedure.

Wright moved to suppress the identification testimony, arguing that the identification procedures were unduly suggestive. The court denied the motion, finding that the identifications were not "overly suggestive and improper" because they were made in a "very short spatial and temporal time between the incident and arrest." Wright was convicted of second-degree robbery and second-degree criminal trespass. The Appellate Division agreed and affirmed the conviction, holding that the show-up procedure used by police was not unduly suggestive. 

**People v Thomas**
New York Court of Appeals
June 18, 2024

*LEO LAW Topic 10-3: Reasonable suspicion of criminal activity is required to extend a traffic stop.*

An off-duty police officer observed Kevin Thomas driving outside his county of residence, violating his parole conditions. The officer contacted an on-duty officer who initiated a traffic stop after observing Thomas commit a traffic infraction. During the stop, Thomas provided inconsistent responses and refused to consent to a search of his vehicle. The police contacted Thomas's parole officer, who arrived at the scene and conducted a warrantless search of Thomas's vehicle, discovering a large quantity of heroin.

The County Court denied Thomas's motion to suppress the evidence, finding that the initial stop was justified and that the police had a "founded suspicion" of criminality, justifying Thomas's continued detention to contact his parole officer. The court also found that the parole officer's search was rationally and reasonably related to his parole duties. Thomas was convicted of criminal possession of a controlled substance in the third degree. The Appellate Division affirmed the decision.

The Court of Appeals reversed the decision, stating that the lower courts applied an incorrect legal standard in analyzing whether the traffic stop was unreasonably prolonged. The court held that reasonable suspicion is the proper standard for detaining an individual beyond the time reasonably required to complete a traffic stop. The court found that the traffic stop was justified at its inception, but the courts below evaluated whether the traffic stop was prolonged beyond the time reasonably required for its completion under the founded suspicion standard, a lesser standard than the reasonable suspicion necessary to prolong a traffic stop. The case was remitted to the County Court for further proceedings under the correct standard.

**State v. Wiskowski**
Wisconsin Supreme Court
June 18, 2024

*LEO LAW Topic 5-31, 10-2: The investigative detention **was **un**supported by reasonable suspicion** and was **also** invalid under the community caretaking function.*

Michael Wiskowski fell asleep in a McDonald's drive-thru lane behind the wheel of his truck. An employee knocked on his window to wake him up and then called the police. Arriving a minute later, a responding officer observed the defendant driving normally without any impairment. However, the officer decided to pull the defendant over due to the report of him falling asleep. The defendant explained that he was tired from a 24-hour shift. Despite not observing any signs of impairment, the officer prolonged the stop to investigate further. The officer eventually ordered the defendant out of his truck, at which point the defendant showed signs of intoxication, leading to his arrest and charges.

The defendant moved to suppress the evidence discovered during the stop. The circuit court denied the motion, concluding that the stop and further investigation were justified as a permissible "community caretaking function." The court of appeals agreed with the lower court's decision.

The Supreme Court of Wisconsin reversed the lower courts' decisions. The court found that the traffic stop was not supported by reasonable suspicion. Furthermore, even if the traffic stop was permissible as a community caretaking activity, the court held that the stop was unreasonably prolonged when it transformed into an unjustified criminal investigation. The court stated that the scope of caretaking stops should be guided and limited by the justification for the stop. This means that absent any other permissible reason to detain someone, the detention must end when the original community caretaking justification is resolved. The court remanded the case to the circuit court with instructions to vacate the judgment of conviction and grant the motion to suppress.

**NOT FOR PUBLICATION**

United States v. Alfonso

Court: US Court of Appeals for the Eleventh Circuit

Dockets: 22-10576, 22-10589, 22-10590

Opinion Date: June 14, 2024

Judge: BRANCH

> **Areas of Law:** Admiralty & Maritime Law, Criminal Law

The United States Coast Guard seized a vessel in the Dominican Republic's Exclusive Economic Zone (EEZ) that bore no nationality indicators. The crew claimed Colombian nationality for the vessel, but Colombia could not confirm or deny the vessel's registry, rendering it stateless under the Maritime Drug Law Enforcement Act (MDLEA). The vessel was found to contain drugs, leading to the arrest and prosecution of the crew members, Jhonathan Alfonso, Jose Jorge Kohen, and Jose Miguel Rosario-Rojas, under the MDLEA.

The defendants moved to dismiss the indictment, arguing that the MDLEA was unconstitutional as applied to them because they were arrested in the EEZ, which they asserted is not part of the "high seas" as defined by customary international law. The district court denied the motion, concluding that it had subject matter jurisdiction under the MDLEA. The defendants subsequently pleaded guilty to conspiracy to possess with intent to distribute a controlled substance while on board a vessel subject to the jurisdiction of the United States.

On appeal, the United States Court of Appeals for the Eleventh Circuit affirmed the district court's decision. The court concluded that the EEZ is part of the "high seas" and thus within Congress's authority under the Felonies Clause. The court also concluded that the defendants could not show any plain error with regard to the MDLEA's definition of a vessel without nationality as including vessels where registry is asserted but cannot be confirmed or denied by the foreign country.
